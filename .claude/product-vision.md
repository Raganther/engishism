# Product Vision

## Knowledge

Engishism is evolving from an ESL games site into a custom syllabus gamification framework for English-language schools. The product direction is to convert a school's syllabus, coursebook unit, handout, or workbook material into classroom-ready lesson/game packs.

The first likely buyers are academic managers, directors of studies, and school owners. Teachers are the primary daily users, but the early sales pitch should speak to school-level value: improving syllabus delivery, saving teacher preparation time, increasing classroom energy, and differentiating the school's academic offer.

The early positioning is a service-product hybrid. The first version should show that syllabus material can be personalised for a school, while the underlying platform makes that adaptation repeatable. Self-serve teacher/admin editing can come later after the internal content-to-game pipeline is efficient.

The classroom philosophy is teacher-led and big-screen first. The shared classroom display should support speaking, discussion, team decisions, and teacher facilitation rather than pulling students onto personal screens for the whole lesson.

Student phone interaction is a later optional layer. Phones may eventually act as buzzers, voting tools, team controllers, or lightweight text-entry devices, but the MVP should not depend on student phones.

The core product principle is "solve once, use everywhere." Syllabus content should be structured once and then reused across multiple game formats. Interaction primitives such as tile movement, reveal/check controls, timers, teams, scoring, image prompts, and voting should be reusable across games rather than rebuilt per activity.

The recommended next product spine is one complete proof loop: grammar book unit or school syllabus material -> structured Unit content -> interactive lesson board -> multiple reusable games. For the current project state, the next build direction is Unit 1 Present Continuous lesson board, then a third game such as Sort It to prove that the shared Tile Tray can power a different game dynamic.
